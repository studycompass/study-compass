Active Recall vs. Spaced Repetition: A Guide

June 28, 2026 · 7 min read · By Study Compass Team

If there are two study strategies that almost every cognitive psychologist agrees on, they are active recall and spaced repetition. They are often mentioned in the same breath, but they are not the same thing — and understanding the difference is the key to using both well. Active recall is the act of retrieving information from memory without looking at the answer: closing the book and trying to explain the concept out loud, answering a flashcard before flipping it, or writing everything you remember about a topic on a blank page. Spaced repetition, on the other hand, is about when you review — intentionally revisiting material at increasing intervals (a day, three days, a week, a month) so you hit your memory just before it would have faded.

The two strategies work beautifully together because they attack the same problem — forgetting — from two different angles. Active recall strengthens the retrieval path to a memory each time you walk it; spaced repetition ensures you walk that path again at the moment it would otherwise start to decay. Hermann Ebbinghaus mapped this decay in the 1880s with his famous forgetting curve, which shows that without review, most newly learned material is lost within days. A simple weekly schedule can fight back: assign new material to active recall on Day 1, revisit it on Day 3, again on Day 7, and once more on Day 14. By the third review, the curve has flattened dramatically and the knowledge has moved toward long-term memory.

The easiest way to put this into practice is to build a small stack of flashcards for each class and review them on a spaced schedule.
